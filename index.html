Style guide

The academic courses my department is responsible for require regular updates to ensure they are fully up-to-date in terms of educational value. One of my major projects revolved around this principle: I was tasked with creating a new style guide for recreating the various graphics in a major computer science course.

These were highly technical illustrations that were originally made without design considerations which was acceptable at the time but I had to elevate them to meet higher expectations with a modern yet timeless, aesthetically pleasing manner while communicating all of the necessary information they have originally shown.

The first step to this goal was to gain an understanding of the graphs. I conducted throughout research of the various graphs used in computer science. As I made a Trello board to categories the various graph and model types as a visual aid for progressing with the design, with guide notes detailing specific technical requirements for the individual types.

After research I made a couple of design to iterate on the test course pages and see what works best for our purposes. Once the design was finalised I created a Style Guide. This was used by the design team to create the new assets.

Accessibility

My mission is to ensure everybody regardless of their abilities can have the equal level of learning opportunity from our courses. In order to achieve this I need to guarantee that our interactives support a high level of accessibility and can be navigated without a mouse, are not reliant on colour only, and have appropriate labels set up for screen readers. To learn more about accessibility I participated in courses on the subject.

Another important accessibility issue I tackled was turning our various pdf documents readable by assistive technology. Often this meant the starting point was scanned book pages without searchable text and thus I learned how to work with character recognition techniques, set up labels and reading order. This is deceiptfully difficult for currently there aren't software and methods readily available for achieving completely accurate results.

Finally I explored accessible svg images. My goal was to convert all graphics if applicable to svg as these support aria labels and searchable text – both requirements for assistive technologies.
